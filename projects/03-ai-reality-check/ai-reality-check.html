AI Capabilities & Limitations —
A Marketing Officer's Reality Check

Ken Tam Marketing Officer, Sydney April 2026 AI Fluency 4D Framework
First-hand observations
Where AI genuinely helped
01
Writing Copy & Captions

Used AI to generate first drafts of Instagram captions, email subject lines, and product descriptions at scale. Generated 10–20 variations in seconds, giving me real options to evaluate.

Strong at matching tone when given clear examples. Significantly reduced time on first drafts — I shifted from writing to editing and deciding, which is actually higher-value work.

THE LESSON

AI is a prolific first-drafter. My role shifted from writing to curating — which turns out to be where the real judgment lives.

02
Analysing Campaign Data

Pasted campaign performance data — engagement rates, reach, CTR — and asked AI to identify trends and anomalies. It quickly spotted patterns I might have taken hours to find manually.

Generated stakeholder-ready summaries and hypotheses for why certain posts outperformed others.

THE LESSON

AI excels at the "so what?" stage of data work. It dramatically reduces time between raw numbers and actionable insight.

03
Brainstorming Creative Ideas

Used AI as a thinking partner for campaign concepts, content pillars, and audience messaging. Even bad ideas helped clarify what I actually wanted.

Best in Augmentation mode — genuine back-and-forth dialogue rather than one-shot requests. The human-AI iteration produced better ideas than either could alone.

THE LESSON

AI is an exceptional brainstorming sparring partner. The best output comes from iteration, not single prompts.

Hard-won lessons
Where AI disappointed me
HALLUCINATION
Confident, but wrong
AI cited fabricated competitor statistics that looked completely credible
What happened

AI provided specific engagement statistics for a competitor brand. The numbers were formatted professionally and cited confidently. They were fabricated. LLMs generate plausible-sounding text — they don't "look things up."

My fix
  • Treat all AI statistics as unverified until confirmed
  • Source specific facts via Semrush, GA, or brand sites
  • Ask AI to flag uncertainty in the prompt itself
BRAND VOICE
Polished but generic
AI wrote competent copy that could have been for any brand in the category
What happened

Asked AI to write in "our brand voice" without further context. The output was technically correct but totally forgettable — AI defaults to the average of the category when not given specific guidance.

My fix
  • Always include 3–5 on-brand examples (few-shot learning)
  • Define tone explicitly in every prompt
  • Maintain a reusable brand voice prompt block
GENERIC OUTPUT
Safe, predictable, forgettable
AI brainstormed campaigns that hit every checkbox but broke no ground
What happened

Campaign ideas felt like a marketing textbook — technically correct, strategically sound, creatively inert. AI is trained on existing content; it excels at producing the average of what exists, not what's genuinely original.

My fix
  • Ask for 10 conventional ideas first, then 5 that break them
  • Bring the unconventional angle; use AI to develop it
  • Use AI output as a constraint to push against
KNOWLEDGE CUTOFF
Doesn't know "now"
Confident analysis of competitor strategy that was 12–18 months out of date
What happened

Asked for a competitor social media analysis. Got a structured, confident answer — based on what the brand was doing over a year ago. AI doesn't know what happened after its training cutoff, but doesn't always say so.

My fix
  • Use Semrush, Sprout Social, Google Trends for current data
  • Use AI for frameworks and historical context only
  • Ask AI to state its knowledge cutoff when relevant

Decision framework
The two questions I ask before every AI task

My Pre-Task Checklist

QUESTION 01
Does this task require current, verified, or proprietary information?
→ If YES: I provide that information myself, or verify AI output against real sources before using it.
QUESTION 02
Does this task require my brand's specific voice, culture, or relationships?
→ If YES: I give AI explicit examples and context before asking it to produce anything.
If both answers are NO — I delegate freely and focus my energy on reviewing the output rather than producing it.
Framework connection

This checklist maps directly to the Delegation competency in the AI Fluency 4D Framework — thoughtfully deciding what work to give AI, what to retain, and how to set up each collaboration for success. Good Delegation isn't just about efficiency; it's about knowing where human judgment is irreplaceable.
